# Job Search Style Guide

Derived from `Zachary_Butler_Resume_2026.pdf` and `Butler_HealthDistrict_cover_letter.pdf`. Use this as the reference for all resume and cover letter writing.

---

## Resume

### Profile / Summary
- Two sentences max. Format: [role identity + years] → [specialization] → [credibility hook].
- Measurable where possible ("10+ years," "major brands and educational institutions").
- No objective statements. No "seeking a role where I can..."

### Experience Bullets
- Action-verb led. Past tense for past roles, present for current.
- One idea per bullet. One to two lines max.
- Quantify every claim that can be quantified: views, subscribers, class sizes, clients, timelines.
- Name the brands and institutions. "Major clients including Google, Sony, and Blizzard" beats "major enterprise clients."
- Don't pad thin roles — fewer strong bullets beats more weak ones.

### Skills Section
- Split into two lists: **Technical Proficiency** (tools, software, platforms) and **Core Competencies** (capabilities, methods, soft skills).
- Tools by name (Premiere Pro, DaVinci Resolve), not categories (video editing software).

### Credential Anchors by Role Type
Adapt which experience gets emphasis depending on what the position is:

| Target role | Lead credential | Supporting anchor |
|---|---|---|
| Education / edtech / instructional media | Cornell CTI (Instructional Media Producer) | Adjunct Instructor at Montevallo |
| Mission-driven / nonprofit / community org | Cornell CTI + documentary feature (SF Black Film Festival) | Health District letter as model |
| Production / branded content | FBE (React Media) — scale, speed | DEFY Media — major brand clients |
| Content strategy / digital media leadership | Lesson Zero Studio (founder framing) | Cornell CTI + FBE |

### Education
- Degree type, dates, institution, field. No GPA, no honors unless directly relevant.
- MA in Film, Television, and New Media Studies (UW-Milwaukee) is the lead credential.
- English Literature BA (Montevallo) supports the writing/teaching side of the profile.

## Cover Letters

### Opening Paragraph — Critical Rule
**Never open with "I am writing to apply for..."** That sentence is wasted space.

Open instead with what draws you to *this specific role or organization*, then name the role. Lead with motivation, not application mechanics.

Good model to aim for:
> *"Public health communication and documentary storytelling have more in common than most people think — both are about making complex, invisible systems feel urgent and human. That's what drew me to the Digital Media Specialist role at the Health District..."*

The Health District letter recovered quickly from its generic opening, but future letters should start stronger.

### Structure: Bold-Lead Competency Paragraphs
This is the strongest structural feature of the Health District letter. Keep it.

Format:
1. **Short, bold, first-person claim.** (One sentence. Scannable. States the competency directly.)
2. Evidence sentence(s): specific role, specific result, specific client/institution.
3. Implication: what that means for the employer, or what it taught you.

Example from the Health District letter:
> **I know how to tell stories that make institutions feel human.** At Cornell University's Center for Teaching Innovation, I produced documentary-style videos about teaching and learning that turned complex institutional topics into stories audiences actually wanted to watch — contributing to a 5x increase in the department's YouTube views and doubling its subscriber base.

Use 2–4 competency paragraphs depending on role complexity. Don't exceed four — the letter should fit one tight page.

### Bridge Paragraph
Between the opening and the competency body, one short bridge that spans your background to their needs. Keep it to 1–2 sentences. Its job is to frame what follows, not to repeat the opening.

### Closing Paragraph
- Address logistics proactively if relevant (location, willingness to relocate, availability).
- State mission alignment specifically — name something real about the org, not generic "your mission inspires me."
- End with an invitation, not a plea: "I would welcome the chance to discuss..." not "Thank you for your consideration."

### Signature
Sign off as **Zach Butler**, not Zachary Butler. More human.

### Voice and Tone
- First person throughout. Contractions are fine (I'm, I've, what's, isn't).
- Confident, not boastful. State claims directly without hedging ("I'm a hands-on producer who works well on small teams" — not "I believe I have experience working on small teams").
- Named specifics everywhere: org names, client names, real numbers. No vague claims.
- No bullet lists in the body. This is prose.
- Avoid corporate/business-bot register. Replace jargon nouns with plain descriptions of what actually happened. Example: instead of "project intake workflows, digital asset management, and client-facing delivery systems," write "how projects came in, how files were organized, how finished work got to clients."
- One tight page. If it runs long, cut from the competency paragraphs, not the opening or close.

### Portfolio / Links
Include `ctrlz.cc` as the work sample link. Place at the bottom before the signature, not in the body.

## Adaptation Checklist (for each new application)
Before sending any application, verify:

- [ ] Opening line is specific to this org — not generic
- [ ] Competency paragraphs match what the job description actually asks for (reorder or swap as needed)
- [ ] Every named credential is the right one for this role type (see anchor table above)
- [ ] Quantified claims present throughout — no unsubstantiated adjectives
- [ ] Mission alignment close references something real and specific about this employer
- [ ] Length: one page, one tight page
- [ ] Signed as Zach Butler
